Bioresorbable Scaffold Outcomes at 5 Years: A Longitudinal Safety Evaluation

Published In Circulation
Publication Date November 2022
Primary DOI 10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.122.060124
Co-Authors Dr. Ayesha Sharma, Tarun Singh

Abstract

"A multi-center, 5-year follow-up study investigating long-term vascular restoration, scaffold thrombosis rates, and target lesion failure in patients receiving second-generation bioresorbable vascular scaffolds compared to standard metallic drug-eluting stents."

Unlike permanent metallic stents, bioresorbable vascular scaffolds (BVS) are designed to provide temporary support to the coronary artery wall and then be fully absorbed by the body. This study presents critical long-term evidence regarding their safety profile and vascular benefits over 5 years of complete bio-absorption.

Introduction & Technology Overview

Metallic stents successfully prevent acute recoil but permanently bind the coronary artery, which limits natural vasomotion and can lead to late restenosis or thrombosis. Bioresorbable scaffolds offer a transient solution, dissolving over time to allow the treated blood vessel to recover its natural tone and physiological responsiveness.

This clinical trial provides key evidence on whether the long-term clinical safety of BVS matches or exceeds traditional metallic drug-eluting stents (DES).

Research Methodology

The trial enrolled a highly controlled patient cohort across several leading cardiac catheterization academies.

  • Patient Group: 850 patients requiring percutaneous coronary intervention.
  • Scaffold Type: Poly-L-lactide (PLLA) bioresorbable scaffold.
  • Follow-up Strategy: Regular clinical evaluations, including intravascular ultrasound (IVUS) and optical coherence tomography (OCT) at interval stages.

Target Lesion Success Rate

Optical coherence tomography confirmed complete degradation of the polymer scaffold without leaving major chemical residues, allowing natural arterial expansion and recovery of vasomotion.

Discussion & Clinical Conclusion

At 5 years, bioresorbable vascular scaffolds demonstrated comparable target lesion success to metallic stents, with the added benefit of complete scaffold absorption. This allows the coronary artery to return to its natural physiological state, supporting the use of BVS in selected patient groups to improve long-term outcomes.
