Gender Disparities in Cardiac Care Delivery: A Retrospective Analysis

Published In The Lancet
Publication Date August 2022
Primary DOI 10.1016/S0140-6736(22)01104-5
Co-Authors Dr. Ayesha Sharma, Minu Gupta

Abstract

"A retrospective observational study tracking differences in diagnostic speeds, clinical referral practices, drug therapy optimization, and 1-year cardiac survival rates between female and male cohorts presenting with acute coronary syndromes."

Cardiovascular disease is often incorrectly viewed as primarily affecting men. This bias leads to systemic diagnostic delays and lower referral rates for key interventional procedures in female cohorts. This paper examines clinical data to highlight these disparities and identify opportunities to improve equity and patient outcomes.

Introduction & Problem Context

Female patients presenting with myocardial infarction often exhibit different symptoms than the classic chest pain typically seen in male patients. These atypical presentationsâ€”such as shortness of breath, fatigue, or jaw painâ€”frequently lead to misdiagnosis in emergency rooms. This delay in recognizing ischemic signs can severely impact survival rates and recovery paths.

This registry-based analysis aims to highlight critical areas where diagnostic protocols can be updated to ensure equal care quality for all patients.

Research Methodology

We analyzed clinical records from a multi-center registry across five major tertiary academies.

  • Patient Records: 15,400 acute coronary syndrome records over 4 years.
  • Variables Tracked: Door-to-ECG time, angiogram referrals, beta-blocker/statin prescribing rates, and 12-month survival rates.
  • Analysis: Multivariable regression to control for age and comorbidities.

The study found that even when controlling for age and diabetes status, female patients were significantly less likely to be discharged with standard guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT).

Proposed Solutions & Clinical Impact

To address these disparities, we must adopt gender-specific diagnostic protocols in emergency triage. This includes educating clinicians on atypical symptoms and implementing standardized angiogram and discharge guidelines to ensure equal, high-quality care for all cardiac patients.
