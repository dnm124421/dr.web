Machine Learning Applications in Early Detection of Myocardial Infarction

Published In Journal of the American College of Cardiology (JACC)
Publication Date June 2023
Primary DOI 10.1016/j.jacc.2023.05.012
Co-Authors Dr. Ayesha Sharma, Rajesh Kumar

Abstract

"This paper details the development and clinical validation of convolutional deep neural networks trained on high-frequency multi-lead ECG registers. The algorithm accurately recognizes sub-clinical ischemia patterns indicative of acute myocardial infarction, significantly outperforming traditional heuristic diagnostics."

Acute Myocardial Infarction (AMI) is highly time-sensitive. Standard ECG criteria like ST-elevation are reliable but often represent later stages of ischemic injury. This study presents a machine learning solution to detect early electrical changes, improving recovery outcomes and door-to-balloon intervals in emergency settings.

Introduction & Clinical Significance

With cardiovascular crises remaining a leading cause of mortality worldwide, reducing diagnosis time is vital. When a coronary artery is occluded, cell necrosis begins within minutes. Machine learning models can analyze complex, non-obvious ECG patterns to identify acute occlusions before standard diagnostic markers appear.

Our collaborative research group sought to develop a model that processes real-time data to help emergency physicians make faster, more accurate reperfusion decisions.

Research Methodology

We developed a custom deep convolutional neural network (CNN) trained on thousands of clinical records.

  • Database: 12,500 patient multi-lead ECG scans from diverse cohorts.
  • Network Structure: A multi-layered CNN optimized to detect subtle ST-T segment anomalies.
  • Validation: Split testing using independent clinical samples.

Clinical Impact & Future Scope

Integrating AI diagnostics into hospital triage systems can significantly improve care delivery. By flagging high-risk ECGs instantly, the model helps reduce time-to-treatment, saving heart muscle tissue and improving patient recovery paths.
